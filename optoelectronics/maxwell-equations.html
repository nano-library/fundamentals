Maxwell's Equations

PRE-MAXWELL FIELD EQUATIONS

\[ \nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0} \tag{1.1} \] \[ \nabla\cdot\mathbf{B} = 0 \tag{1.2} \] \[ \nabla\times\mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} \tag{1.3} \] \[ \nabla\times\mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J} \tag{1.4} \]

MAXWELL'S EQUATIONS

All Electromagnetic phenomena can be said to follow from Maxwell's equations. These equations are based on experimental observations and are given by the following equations:

\[ \nabla\cdot\mathbf{D} = \rho \tag{1.5} \] \[ \nabla\cdot\mathbf{B} = 0 \tag{1.6} \] \[ \nabla\times\mathbf{E} = -\dfrac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} = -\mu\dfrac{\partial \mathbf{H}}{\partial t} \tag{1.7} \] \[ \nabla\times\mathbf{H} = \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{D}}{\partial t} + \mathbf{J} \tag{1.8} \]

Where \(\rho\) represents the charge density and \(\mathbf{J}\) the current density, \(\mathbf{E}\), \(\mathbf{D}\), \(\mathbf{B}\) and \(\mathbf{H}\) represent the electric field, electric displacement, magnetic induction and magnetic field, respectively.

Equations (1.5)-(1.8) can be solved only if the 'constitutive relations' are known whiich relate \(\mathbf{D}\) to \(\mathbf{E}\), \(\mathbf{B}\) to \(\mathbf{H}\) and \(\mathbf{J}\) to \(\mathbf{E}\).

The 'constitutive relations' depend on the properties of the properties of the medium, field strengths, etc.

For a linear, isotropic and homogeneous medium the 'constitutive relations' are given by the following equations:

\[ \mathbf{D} = \varepsilon \mathbf{E} \tag{1.9} \] \[ \mathbf{B} = \mu \mathbf{H} \tag{1.10} \] \[ \mathbf{J} = \sigma \mathbf{E} \tag{1.11} \]

where \(\varepsilon\), \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\) denote respectively the dielectric permittivity, magnetic permeability and conductivity of the medium respectively.

MAXWELL'S EQUATIONS IN A CHARGE AND CURRENT FREE DIELECTRIC MEDIUM

For a charge free dielectric, we may write

\[ \rho = 0, \quad \text{and} \quad \mathbf{J} = 0 \tag{1.12} \]

Further, in most cases

\[ \mu \approx \mu_0 \]

where

\[ \mu_0 = 4 \pi \times 10^{-7} \; Ns^2 . C^2 \tag{1.13} \]

represents the magnetic permeability of vaccum.

If we use the above relations, Maxwell's equations simplify to

\[ \nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = 0 \tag{1.14} \] \[ \nabla\cdot\mathbf{H} = 0 \tag{1.15} \] \[ \nabla\times\mathbf{E} = -\mu \frac{\partial \mathbf{H}}{\partial t} \tag{1.16} \] \[ \nabla\times\mathbf{H} = \varepsilon \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t} \tag{1.17} \]

MAXWELL'S EQUATIONS IN A CONDUCTING MEDIUM

With free charge density \(\rho_f\), Maxwell's equations take the form:

\[ \nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = \frac{\rho_f}{\varepsilon_0} \tag{1.18} \] \[ \nabla\cdot\mathbf{B} = 0 \tag{1.19} \] \[ \nabla\times\mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} \tag{1.20} \] \[ \nabla\times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J} + \mu \varepsilon \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t} \tag{1.21} \]
