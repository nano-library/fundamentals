2. Electromagnetic Waves

PLANE WAVE ALONG Z-DIRECTION

Suppose the wave travels along \(z\). Then amplitude is constant on planes of constant \(z\):

\[ E_s = E_s(z) \]

The Helmholtz equation reduces to:

\[ \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{E_s}^2}{\partial z^2} + k^2 E_s = 0 \]

with

\[ k = \omega \sqrt{\varepsilon \mu} \]

Solution:

\[ E_s(z) = E_0 e^{\mp i k z} \]

The sign tells direction:

  • \(e^{- i k z}\): wave moving in \(+z\) direction
  • \(e^{+ i k z}\): wave moving in \(-z\) direction

The full field becomes:

\[ E(z,t) = E_0 e^{i(kz - \omega t)} \]

This is a traveling wave.

THE THREE DIMENSIONAL WAVE EQUATION IN A DIELECTRIC

The wave equation can be derived if we take curl of equation (1.16), we would obtain

\[ \nabla\times\nabla\times\mathbf{E} = -\mu \frac{\partial \nabla\times\mathbf{H}}{\partial t} = -\varepsilon \mu \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{E}^2}{\partial t^2} \tag{2.1} \]

where we have used Equation (1.17)

Now, the operator \(\nabla^2\mathbf{E}\) is defined by the following equation:

\[ \nabla^2\mathbf{E} \equiv \nabla(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}) - \nabla\times\nabla\times\mathbf{E} \tag{2.2} \]

Using Cartesian coordinates, one can show that

\[ (\nabla^2\mathbf{E})_x = \nabla(\nabla \cdot E_x) = \frac{\partial^2 E_x^2}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 E_y^2}{\partial y^2} + \frac{\partial^2 E_z^2}{\partial z^2} \]

i.e, a Cartesian component of \(\nabla^2\mathbf{E}\) is the div grad of the Carterisan Component. However, \((\nabla^2\mathbf{E})_r \neq \text{grad div} E_r\). Thus, using

\[ \nabla\times\nabla\times\mathbf{E} = \nabla(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}) - \nabla^2\mathbf{E} \]

we obtain

\[ \nabla(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}) - \nabla^2\mathbf{E} - \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{E}^2}{\partial t^2} \tag{2.3} \]

or

\[ \nabla^2\mathbf{E} = \varepsilon \mu \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{E}^2}{\partial t^2} \tag{2.4} \]

where we have used the Equation \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = 0\), (1.14).

Equation (2.4) is known as the three-dimensional wave equation and each Carterisan component of \(\mathbf{E}\) satisfies the scale wave equation. (Wave Equation Concept)

\[ \nabla^2\mathbf{\psi} = \varepsilon \mu \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{\psi}^2}{\partial t^2} \tag{2.5} \]

The velocity of propagation (\(v\)) of the wave is simply given by

\[ v = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\varepsilon \mu}} \tag{2.6} \]

In a similar manner, one can derive the wave equation satisfied by \(\mathbf{H}\).

\[ \nabla^2\mathbf{H} = \varepsilon \mu \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{H}^2}{\partial t^2} \tag{2.7} \]

It can be seen that the solutions expressed by Equation () and () or Eq () and () indeed satisfy Equations (2.4) and (2.7) provided

\[ \frac{\omega}{k} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\varepsilon \mu}} \tag{2.8} \]

which is the speed of propagation of the electromagnetic wave.

WAVE EQUATION IN A CONDUCTING MEDIUM

In a dielectric, the wave equation is:

\[ \nabla^2 E = \mu \varepsilon \dfrac{\partial^2 E}{\partial t^2} \]

In a conductor,

\[ \nabla^2 E = \mu \varepsilon \dfrac{\partial^2 E}{\partial t^2} + \mu \sigma \dfrac{\partial E}{\partial t} \]

MONOCHROMATIC (SINGLE FREQUENCY) SOLUTION

Assume a time-harmonic form:

\[ \mathbf{E}(r,t) = E_s(r) e^{- i \omega t} \]

Time derivatives:

\[ \dfrac{\partial^2}{\partial t^2} e^{- i \omega t} = - \omega^2 e^{- i \omega t} \]

Substituting into the wave equation removes time completely:

\[ \nabla^2 E_s + \omega^2 \mu_0 E_s = 0 \]

This is the Helmholtz equation.

MAGNETIC FIELD RELATION

From Maxwell's Equation

\[ \nabla \times \mathbf{E} = i \omega \mathbf{B} \]

Carrying out the curl gives:

\[ B_s = \pm \sqrt{\mu \varepsilon} E_s \]

So:

  • \(\mathbf{B}\) is perpendicular to both \(\mathbf{E}\) and propagation direction
  • \[ |B| = \frac{|E|}{v} \]

The magnetic field is not independent. It is generated automatically by the electric field's motion.

REFRACTIVE INDEX

\[ n = \frac{c}{v} \]

Using

\[ v = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\varepsilon \mu}} \]

Then

\[ n = \sqrt{\frac{\varepsilon}{\varepsilon_0}} \]

So refractive index is really a measure of how much the medium allows electric field polarization.
