Random Walk

Consider a particle restricted to one dimension. It starts at the origin and, at each step, moves one unit either to the right or to the left.

Let \( p \) be the probability of moving one unit to the right, and \( q \) the probability of moving one unit to the left.

\[ p + q = 1 \]

Let \( N \) be the total number of steps. Let \( n_1 \) be the number of steps to the right and \( n_2 \) the number of steps to the left.

\[ N = n_1 + n_2 \tag{1} \]

Define a random variable \( s_i \) for the \( i^{\text{th}} \) step:

\[ s_i = \begin{cases} +1 & \text{for Right (R)} \\ -1 & \text{for Left (L)} \end{cases} \]

The total displacement after \( N \) steps is

\[ m = \sum_{i=1}^{N} s_i \]

The final position is

\[ x = m \cdot l \qquad \text{or} \qquad m = n_1 - n_2 \tag{2} \]

Using equations (1) and (2),

\[ m = n_1 - (N - n_1) = 2n_1 - N \]

The probability of one ordered sequence containing \( n_1 \) right steps and \( n_2 \) left steps is

\[ p^{n_1} \cdot q^{n_2} \]

The number of distinct sequences with \( n_1 \) right steps and \( n_2 \) left steps is

\[ \frac{N!}{n_1! \, n_2!} \]

Therefore, the total probability of obtaining exactly \( n_1 \) right steps is

\[ P(n_1) = \frac{N!}{n_1! \, n_2!} \cdot p^{n_1} \cdot q^{n_2} \] \[ P(n_1) = \frac{N!}{n_1! (N - n_1)!} \cdot p^{n_1} \cdot q^{\,N - n_1} \]

This is the binomial distribution for a one-dimensional random walk.

Probability of Total Left Steps

Similarly, the total probability of obtaining exactly \( n_2 \) left steps is

\[ P(n_2) = \frac{N!}{n_1! \, n_2!} \cdot p^{n_1} \cdot q^{n_2} \]

Using \( n_1 = N - n_2 \),

\[ P(n_2) = \frac{N!}{(N - n_2)! \, n_2!} \cdot p^{\,N - n_2} \cdot q^{n_2} \]

Thus, the distribution of left steps is also binomial. It is simply the same distribution viewed from the complementary variable, since \( n_1 + n_2 = N \).
