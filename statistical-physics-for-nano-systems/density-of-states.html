Density of States

The term \(\omega (E)\) is defined as the number of accessible states per unit energy. In large systems, the degeneracy of each energy level is huge and neighboring energy levels are very tightly packed. The spacing between the levels for the air in a room is about \(10^{-24}\) eV, or about \(10^{-50}\) of the total internal energy of the air. This is far too small to be measurable.

When dealing with large systems, then, we cannot control the particular quantum energy for the system as a whole. The best we can do is to hold the system to within some small energy range \(\delta E\), which is very large in comparison with the spacing of the levels.

For the number of states in the range between \(E\) and \(E + \delta E\).

\[ \Omega (E) = \omega (E) \delta E \tag{5.1} \]

where the 'density of states', \(\omega (E)\), is the number of states per unit energy.

\(\omega (E)\) depends on \(\delta E\), is defined number of states per unit energy.

\[ \Omega (E) \propto \delta E \tag{5.2} \]

NUMBER OF STATES AND ENERGY

How does number of states \(\Omega\) depend on energy \(E\). What is the functional form?

FUNCTIONAL FORM FOR A PARTICLE IN A BOX

For a single particle of mass \(m\) in a 3D box of volume \(V\), the energy and momentum are related by

\[ E = \frac{P^2}{2m} \tag{5.3} \]

Momentum Space Volume: The condition that a particle has energy less than or equal to \(E\) is equivalent to its momentum lying within a sphere of radius \(P = \sqrt{2mE}\) in momentum space.

The total energy of any particle is the sum of its Kinetic Energy (\(K\)) and its Potential Energy (\(U\)).

\[ H = K + U \tag{5.4} \]

Because the particle is in a 3D box, it can move along the x, y, and z axes. Its total momentum squared is the sum of the squares of its components:

\[ P^2 = P_x^2 + P_y^2 + P_z^2 \tag{5.5} \]

Now, we get

\[ H = \frac{P^2_x}{2m} + \frac{P^2_y}{2m} + \frac{P^2_z}{2m} + U \tag{5.6} \]

Since the potential \(U=0\) inside the box, the Hamiltonian simplifies strictly to the kinetic energy terms.

\[ H = \frac{P^2_x}{2m} + \frac{P^2_y}{2m} + \frac{P^2_z}{2m} \tag{5.7} \]

If we set, \(H \leq E\), the equation defines the geometry of the system. States with energy less than or equal to \(E\) should satisfy

\[ \frac{P^2_x + P^2_y + P^2_z}{2m} \leq E \tag{5.8} \] \[ P^2_x + P^2_y + P^2_z \leq 2mE \]

The equation is mathematically identical to the equation of a sphere in momentum space with a radius of \(P = \sqrt{2mE}\)

\[ P^2_x + P^2_y + P^2_z \leq (\sqrt{2mE})^2 \]

Let \(\phi(E)\) be states with energy less than or equal to \(E\).

For a particle confined within a physical volume \(V\), the number of states with energy less than or equal to \(E\) is directly proportional to the volume of the corresponding sphere in momentum space

\[ V = \frac{4\pi}{3} P^3, \quad \text{where} \; P = \sqrt{2mE} \tag{5.9} \]

Consequently, as the volume of this sphere in momentum space increases, there is a commensurate increase in the multiplicity of available microstates \(\phi(E)\)

\[ \phi(E) \propto V \] \[ \phi(E) \propto (\sqrt{2mE})^3 \] \[ \phi(E) \propto E^{\frac{3}{2}} \tag{5.10} \]

We want to know how many states exist in a tiny window (\(\delta E\)) around a specific energy level.

  • \(\phi (E + \delta E)\): is the volume of a sphere with a slightly larger radius.
  • \(\phi (E)\)is the volume of the original sphere.

The difference between them, \(\Omega (E)\), is the volume of the shell.

\[ \Omega (E) = \phi(E + \delta E) - \phi(E) \tag{5.11} \]

For a very small (infinitesimal) (\(\delta E\)), we can rearrange this to find the change in the number of states:

\[ \phi(E + \delta E) - \phi(E) \approx \dfrac{d \phi}{d E} \cdot \delta E \]

we get the fundamental relation,

\[ \Omega (E) = \dfrac{d \phi}{d E} \cdot \delta E \tag{5.12} \]

From equation (5.10),

\[ \dfrac{d \phi}{d E} \propto \frac{3}{2}E^{\frac{3}{2}-1} = \frac{3}{2}E^{\frac{1}{2}} \] \[ \Omega (E) = \frac{3}{2}E^{\frac{1}{2}} \cdot \delta E \tag{5.13} \]

Since \(\delta E\) is just a constant width,

\[ \Omega (E) \propto E^{\frac{1}{2}} \tag{5.14} \]

DENSITY OF STATES FOR N-PARTICLES IN 3N-DIMENSIONAL SPACE

A system of N particles in 3D has \(f = 3N\) degrees of freedom. The Hamiltonian for \(N\) particles is

\[ H = \sum_{i=1}^{3N} \frac{P_i^2}{2m} \tag{5.15} \]

The condition that the total energy is less than or equal to \(E\) is

\[ \sum_{i=1}^{3N} \frac{P_i^2}{2m} \leq E \tag{5.16} \]

This is the equation for a ball in \(3N\)-dimensions with radius, \(R = \sqrt{2mE}\).

\[ \sum P_i^2 \leq (\sqrt{2mE})^2 \]

In geometry, the volume of a D-dimensional sphere always scales with the radius to the power of the dimension (\(R^D\)). Here, \(f = 3N\) degrees of freedom

Let \(\phi(E)\) be states with energy less than or equal to \(E\).

\[ \phi (E) \propto R^{3N} \propto (E^{\frac{1}{2}})^{3N} = E^{\frac{3N}{2}} \tag{5.17} \]

This \(\phi(E)\) represents the total volume of that 3N-dimensional ball.

Just like the single-particle case, we take the derivative to find the number of states in the thin shell at the surface.

\[ \dfrac{d \phi}{d E} \propto \dfrac{d }{d E}(E^{\frac{3N}{2}}) = \frac{3N}{2} E^{\frac{3N}{2} - 1} \] \[ \Omega (E) \propto \frac{3N}{2}E^{\left(\frac{3N}{2} - 1 \right)} \tag{5.18} \]

When N is very large (e.g., \(10^{23}\) particles), the term \(E^{\left(\frac{3N}{2} - 1 \right)}\) is dominated by \(\frac{3N}{2}\),

\[ \Omega (E) \approx E^{\left(\frac{3N}{2} - 1 \right)} \tag{5.19} \]

Using Boltzmann's formula:

\[ S = k \ln \Omega \tag{5.20} \] \[ S \propto k \ln \left(E^{\left(\frac{3N}{2} - 1 \right)}\right) \] \[ S \propto \frac{3N}{2} k \ln E \] \[ S \propto N \ln E \tag{5.21} \]
