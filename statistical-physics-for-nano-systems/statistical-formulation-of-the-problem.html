Statistical Formulation of the Problem

1. Specification of the State of the System

(a) Classical system:
The state of a particle is specified by its position and momentum. A point in phase space represents one complete microscopic state.

(b) Quantum system:
Position and momentum cannot be specified simultaneously with arbitrary precision. They are constrained by the uncertainty relation

\[ \delta x \, \delta p \ge \frac{\hbar}{2} \]

A quantum state is described by a wavefunction

\[ \psi(q_1, q_2, \ldots, q_f) \]

where \( f \) is the number of degrees of freedom.

2. Statistical Ensemble

Knowing \( \psi(t_0) \) determines \( \psi(t) \) in principle. But tracking \( \psi \) for \( N \sim 10^{23} \) particles is practically impossible. Hence we use statistical descriptions.

3. Basic Postulate of A Priori Probability

An isolated system in equilibrium is equally likely to be in any of its accessible microstates.

4. Probability Calculations

Particle in a One-Dimensional Box

\[ \psi_n = \sqrt{\frac{2}{L}} \sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right), \quad n = 1,2,3,\ldots \]

Each value of \( n \) corresponds to a distinct microstate.

Microstate

A microstate is a complete microscopic specification of a system according to quantum mechanics.

Label all possible quantum states as

\[ r = 1,2,3,\ldots \]

Example: Three Spin-\(\frac{1}{2}\) Particles in a Magnetic Field

Each particle has magnetic moment \( \mu \) along the \( z \)-axis. Placed in an external magnetic field \( H \) along \( +z \).

\[ E = -\vec{\mu} \cdot \vec{H} \]
r State \(E_r\)
1 \(\uparrow \uparrow \uparrow\) \(-3\mu H\)
2 \(\downarrow \uparrow \uparrow\) \(-\mu H\)
3 \(\uparrow \downarrow \uparrow\) \(-\mu H\)
4 \(\uparrow \uparrow \downarrow\) \(-\mu H\)
5 \(\downarrow \downarrow \uparrow\) \(+\mu H\)
6 \(\uparrow \downarrow \downarrow\) \(+\mu H\)
7 \(\downarrow \uparrow \downarrow\) \(+\mu H\)
8 \(\downarrow \downarrow \downarrow\) \(+3\mu H\)

States 2, 3, 4 are degenerate. States 5, 6, 7 are degenerate.

Statistical Ensemble

Basic Postulate

All accessible microstates of an isolated equilibrium system are equally probable.

Probability Calculations

Consider an isolated system with energy in the range \( E \) to \( E + \delta E \).

\[ \Omega(E) = \text{total number of accessible states} \] \[ \Omega(E, y_k) = \text{number of states where parameter } y = y_k \]

Probability that parameter \( y \) assumes value \( y_k \):

\[ P(y_k) = \frac{\Omega(E, y_k)}{\Omega(E)} \]

Mean value of \( y \):

\[ \bar{y} = \frac{\sum_k \Omega(E, y_k)\, y_k} {\Omega(E)} \]

Spin Probability Question

If total energy \( E_{\text{total}} = -\mu H \), this corresponds to the three degenerate states 2, 3, and 4.

Among these three states, the first particle is spin-up in two cases (3 and 4).

\[ P_{\uparrow} = \frac{2}{3} \]

Mean Magnetic Moment of the First Spin

For the first particle:

\[ \mu = \begin{cases} +\mu & \text{(spin up)} \\ -\mu & \text{(spin down)} \end{cases} \]

Thus,

\[ \bar{\mu} = (+\mu)\left(\frac{2}{3}\right) + (-\mu)\left(\frac{1}{3}\right) = \frac{\mu}{3} \]

The average magnetic moment of the first spin in this energy ensemble is

\[ \boxed{\bar{\mu} = \frac{\mu}{3}} \]
