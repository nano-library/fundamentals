Accounting Elements

Capital Expenditure

Capital Expenditure is an expenditure incurred during an accounting period, the benefits of which will be available for more than one accounting period. It includes any expenditure resulting in the acquisition of a fixed asset or contributing to the revenue earning capacity of the business. It is non-recurring in nature.

Features:

  1. It gives benefit for more than one accounting period.
  2. It includes acquisition of fixed assets and all expenditure incurred up to the point an asset is ready to use.
  3. It contributes to the revenue earning capacity of the business.
  4. It is non-recurring in nature.
  5. It is shown on the assets side of the balance sheet.

Examples:

  • Cost of acquisition of land and building.
  • Cost of acquisition of office equipment and computers.
  • Cost of acquisition of plant and machinery including installation charges.

Revenue Expenditure and Deferred Revenue Expenditure

Revenue Expenditure : Revenue expenditure is the expenditure incurred for day-to-day running of the business or for maintaining the earning capacity of the business. It is recurring in nature and is incurred to generate revenue for a particular accounting period.

Features of Revenue Expenditure:

  1. It is recurring in nature.
  2. It is incurred for maintaining the earning capacity of the business.
  3. Its benefit expires within the same accounting period.
  4. It is shown in the Profit and Loss Account.

Examples of Revenue Expenditure:

  • Purchase of goods for resale.
  • Administrative, selling and distribution expenses.
  • Manufacturing expenses.

Deferred Revenue Expenditure : Deferred revenue expenditure is an expenditure which is revenue in nature, but the benefit of which is to be derived over a subsequent period or periods.

  • It is not fully written off in the year of actual expenditure. It is written off over a period of certain years.
  • The balance available after writing off (i.e., Actual expenditure - Amount written off) is shown on the assets side of the Balance Sheet.

Examples:

  • Heavy advertisement expenses.

Examples of Capital, Revenue and Deferred Revenue Expenditure:

  • Capital Expenditure: Purchase of land, building, plant and machinery; installation charges; major repairs increasing capacity.
  • Revenue Expenditure: Maintenance expenses; purchase of food materials; wages and salaries; advertising; administrative expenses.
  • Deferred Revenue Expenditure: Heavy advertisement expenditure spread over multiple years.

Revenue Receipts and Capital Receipts

Revenue Receipts : Receipts which are obtained in the normal course of business are called revenue receipts. They are recurring in nature and the amount received is generally small.

Examples of Revenue Receipts:

  • Proceeds from sale of goods.
  • Interest on investments received.
  • Rent received.
  • Dividend from investment in shares.
  • Commission received.
  • Sale of old newspapers.
  • Bad debts recovered.

Capital Receipts : Capital receipts are those receipts which are not obtained in the normal course of business. They are non-recurring in nature and usually involve large amounts. Capital receipts either create a liability or reduce an asset of the business. They are shown in the Balance Sheet.

Examples of Capital Receipts:

  • Life membership fee.
  • Special donations received.
  • Loan borrowed.
  • Sale of fixed assets.
  • Contribution towards capital fund.
  • Government grants.
  • Long-term investments sold.
