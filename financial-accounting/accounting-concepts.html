Accounting Concepts

Accounting concepts refer to the basic assumptions, rules and principles which form the basis for recording business transactions and preparing accounts.

The main objective of accounting concepts is to maintain uniformity and consistency in accounting records. These concepts constitute the very foundation of accounting. They have been developed over the years from practical experience and are universally accepted rules.

Basic Concepts of Accounting

1. Business Entity Concept

According to this concept, business is treated as a unit or entity separate from its owners, creditors and others. The proprietor of a business concern is considered distinct from the business which he controls. All business transactions are recorded in the books of accounts from the viewpoint of the business. Even the proprietor is treated as a creditor to the extent of his capital.

2. Money Measurement Concept

Under this concept, only those business transactions and events which are of financial nature and can be expressed in terms of money are recorded in the books of accounts. Transactions which cannot be measured in money terms are not recorded.

3. Going Concern Concept

According to this concept, it is assumed that the business will continue to exist for a long period and there is neither the intention nor the necessity to wind up the business in the foreseeable future.

4. Accounting Period Concept

As the life of a business is indefinite, its financial results are divided into artificial time periods to know the operating results and financial position of the business. Usually, one year is considered as the accounting period.

5. Accounting Cost (Historical Cost) Concept

Under this concept, assets are recorded at the price paid to acquire them. This cost becomes the basis for all subsequent accounting for the asset, irrespective of changes in its market value.

6. Duality Aspect Concept

This concept is the basis of the Double Entry System of book-keeping. Every business transaction has two aspects: receiving benefit and giving benefit. For example, when an asset is acquired, cash is paid for it.

7. Realisation Concept

According to this concept, revenue is considered as income only when it is realised. Unearned or unrealised revenue is not taken into account. It helps in determining income pertaining to a particular accounting period.

8. Accrual Concept

Under this concept, income and expenses are recorded when they are earned or incurred, not when cash is received or paid. It ensures that all revenues and expenses relating to a particular accounting period are properly recorded.

9. Matching Concept

According to this concept, the revenues earned during an accounting period are matched with the expenses incurred to earn those revenues. This helps in determining the correct profit or loss for the period.
