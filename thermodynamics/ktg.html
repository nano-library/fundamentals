Microscopic Model for an Ideal Gas

Ideal gas

The ideal gas have the following properties:

- The gas molecules do not interact with each other.

- The volume of each molecule is negligible.

- The molecules have elastic collision with wall of the container.

Boyle's Law

$$ V \propto \frac{1}{P} $$

Charle's Law

$$ V \propto T $$ $$ V \propto n $$ $$ P \propto n $$

Equation of ideal gas

$$ PV = nRT \tag{1} $$

Kinetic Molecular Theory

Consider a single molecule bouncing back and forth in a container of volume \(V\).

\[ V = A \cdot L \]

where \(A\) is area of piston and \(L\) is width of the container.

The average pressure exerted by the single molecule on the piston.

\[ \bar{P} = \frac{\bar{F}_x}{A} \]

where \(F_x\) is force excerted on the piston.

By Newton's third law,

\[ \bar{P} = \frac{-\bar{F}_x}{A} \]

Now, \(F_x\) is the force excerted by the piston on the molecule.

\[ \bar{P} = \frac{-m \left(\frac{\Delta v_x}{\Delta t}\right)}{A} \tag{2} \] The particle bounces and moves in opposite direction (\(-x\)) by elastic collision. \[ \Delta \bar{v}_x = -(\bar{v}_x)_{\text{final}} - (\bar{v}_x)_{\text{initial}} = -2\bar{v}_x \tag{3} \]

Consider '\(\Delta t\)' to be time for one period for the collision to occur.

The distance travelled by the particle is \(2L\).

\[ \Delta t = \frac{2L}{\bar{v}_x} \tag{4} \] Substituting equation (3) and (4) in equation (2) \[ \bar{P} = \frac{m \cdot \bar{v}_x}{A \cdot L} \] since volume is area of piston times width of the container. \[ \bar{P} = \frac{m \cdot \bar{v}_x}{V} \] Multiplying both sides by volume. \[ \bar{P}V = m \cdot \bar{v}_x \]

Now that is derived for only single molecule.

For 'N' number of molecules

\[ \bar{P}V = m \cdot \bar{v}_{1 x} + m \cdot \bar{v}_{2 x} +.... \]

For large 'N',

\[ \bar{P}V = N \cdot m \cdot \bar{v}_x \tag{6} \] From the Ideal Gas Law in its microscopic form, \[ PV = Nk_BT \tag{7} \] Substituting Equation (6) in Equation (7) gives us, \[ Nm\bar{v}_x = Nk_BT \] \[ m\bar{v}_x = k_BT \] Rewritting it as \[ \frac{1}{2}m\bar{v}_x = \frac{1}{2}k_BT \]

The left had side is kinetic energy in x-direction.

\[ KE = \frac{1}{2}k_BT \] Similarly for a molecule moving along only y-direction and z-direction \[ \frac{1}{2}m\bar{v}_y = \frac{1}{2}k_BT \] \[ \frac{1}{2}m\bar{v}_z = \frac{1}{2}k_BT \] Total translation kinetic energy of molecule moving along all three directions \[ KE = \frac{1}{2}m(v_x^2 + v_y^2 + v_z^2), \] \[ KE_{\text{translational}} = \frac{3}{2}k_BT \]
