Phase Transition

Different physical states of a system are called phases. A phase transition is a discontinuous change in thermodynamic properties when an external parameter such as temperature or pressure is varied infinitesimally.

Examples:

  • \(H_2O\): ice → liquid water → water vapour
  • Carbon: diamond, graphite, graphene

Heating Curve (Temperature vs Time)

When ice is heated uniformly:

  • Temperature rises until \(0^\circ C\)
  • At \(0^\circ C\), temperature remains constant during melting (latent heat of fusion)
  • Temperature rises again until \(100^\circ C\)
  • At \(100^\circ C\), temperature remains constant during boiling (latent heat of vaporization)
  • Then vapour temperature increases

During phase change, heat is absorbed but temperature remains constant because energy goes into changing molecular configuration rather than kinetic energy.

Latent Heat

\[ Q = mL \]

where \(L\) is latent heat (fusion or vaporization).

Phase Diagram of \(H_2O\)

Triple Point

The triple point is the unique combination of temperature and pressure where all three phases coexist in equilibrium.

\[ T_{\text{triple}} = 273.16\,\text{K}, \quad P_{\text{triple}} \approx 611\,\text{Pa} \]

Critical Point

The critical point is the end of the liquid–vapour boundary. Beyond this point, liquid and vapour become indistinguishable.

\[ T_c = 647\,\text{K}, \quad P_c \approx 22.1\,\text{MPa} \]

Above \(T_c\), the substance becomes a supercritical fluid. There is no phase boundary.

Important Feature of Water

The solid–liquid boundary has a negative slope. This means increasing pressure lowers the melting point. That is why ice can melt under pressure.

Other Examples of Phase Transitions

Type I Superconductor

At critical temperature \(T_c\), electrical resistance drops abruptly to zero. This is a phase transition between normal and superconducting states.

Magnetic Phase Transition

At Curie temperature \(T_C\), a ferromagnet becomes paramagnetic. Magnetization drops continuously to zero.

Allotropes of Carbon

Diamond, graphite, and graphene are different structural phases of carbon, stable under different pressure–temperature conditions.

Gibbs Phase Rule

The Gibbs phase rule tells us how many independent intensive variables can be changed without altering the number of phases in equilibrium.

\[ F = C - P + 2 \]

Where:

  • \(F\) = degrees of freedom
  • \(C\) = number of components
  • \(P\) = number of phases

Example: Single Component System (\(H_2O\))

\(C = 1\)

1. Single Phase (\(P=1\))
\[ F = 1 - 1 + 2 = 2 \]

Two variables (T and P) can be changed independently.

2. Two Phases Coexisting (\(P=2\))
\[ F = 1 - 2 + 2 = 1 \]

Only one variable can be chosen independently. For example, if temperature is fixed, pressure is automatically determined.

3. Triple Point (\(P=3\))
\[ F = 1 - 3 + 2 = 0 \]

No freedom. Temperature and pressure are fixed. The system is completely constrained.
