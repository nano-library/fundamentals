Doolittle's Factorisation

A matrix A is said to have a Doolittle factorization if there exists a unit lower triangular matrix \(L\) and an upper triangular matrix \(U\) such that \(A = LU\).

For Doolittle LU without pivoting (i.e., \(A=LU\) with \(L\) having 1's on the diagonal, and no row swaps), a standard sufficient condition is:

\[ D_1 \neq 0, D_2 \neq 0, \ldots, D_n \neq = 0 \]

Note:

1. Inverse of lower triangular matrix is a lower trianglar matrix.

2. Inverse of upper trianglar matrix is a upper triangular matrix.

Theorem: Doolittle Factorisation is Unique

Proof:

Let \(A = LU\), \(|A| \ne 0\), where \(L\) is a unit lower triangular matrix, \(|L|= 1\) and \(U\) is a upper trianglar matrix, \(U = u_{11}u_{22}\ldots u_{nn} \ne 0\)

Let \(A = L_1U_1 = L_2U_2, \; L_1 ne L_2, \; U_1 \ne U_2\)

We have

\[ L_1U_1 = L_2U_2 \] \[ L_2^{-1}(L_1U_1) = L_2^{-1}(L_2U_2) \] \[ (L_2^{-1}L_1)U_1 = (L_2^{-1}L_2)U_2 \] \[ L_2^{-1}L_2 = I \] \[ (L_2^{-1}L_1)U_1 = U_2 \] \[ (L_2^{-1}L_1)U_1U_1^{-1} = U_2U_1^{-1} \] \[ U_1U_1^{-1} = I \] \[ L_2^{-1}L_1 = U_2U_1^{-1} = I \] \[ L_2^{-1}L_1 = I \quad \text{and} \quad U_2U_1^{-1} = I \] \[ L_2 = L_1 \quad \text{and} \quad U_2 = U_1 \]

Example 1

Find the Doolittle LU factorization of the following matrix.

\[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 & 2 \\ 2 & 5 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 & 6 \end{bmatrix}. \]

Solution

Step 1: Check leading principal minors

The leading principal minors are:

\[ D_1 = \det[4] = 4 \ne 0, \] \[ D_2 = \det \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 \\ 2 & 5 \end{bmatrix} = (4)(5) - (2)(2) = 20 - 4 = 16 \ne 0, \] \[ D_3 = \det(A) = \det \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 & 2 \\ 2 & 5 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 & 6 \end{bmatrix}. \]

Expanding \(D_3\) along the first row:

\[ D_3 = 4 \begin{vmatrix} 5 & 1 \\ 1 & 6 \end{vmatrix} - 2 \begin{vmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 2 & 6 \end{vmatrix} + 2 \begin{vmatrix} 2 & 5 \\ 2 & 1 \end{vmatrix}. \]

Computing the \(2\times2\) determinants:

\[ \begin{vmatrix} 5 & 1 \\ 1 & 6 \end{vmatrix} = (5)(6) - (1)(1) = 30 - 1 = 29, \] \[ \begin{vmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 2 & 6 \end{vmatrix} = (2)(6) - (1)(2) = 12 - 2 = 10, \] \[ \begin{vmatrix} 2 & 5 \\ 2 & 1 \end{vmatrix} = (2)(1) - (5)(2) = 2 - 10 = -8. \]

Thus,

\[ D_3 = 4(29) - 2(10) + 2(-8) = 116 - 20 - 16 = 80 \ne 0. \]

Since all leading principal minors are non-zero, the matrix \(A\) admits an LU factorization without row exchanges.

Step 2: Define the matrices

In the Doolittle factorization, we seek matrices \(L\) and \(U\) such that \(A = LU\), where \(L\) is lower triangular with diagonal entries equal to 1, and \(U\) is upper triangular.

\[ L = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ \ell_{21} & 1 & 0 \\ \ell_{31} & \ell_{32} & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad U = \begin{bmatrix} u_{11} & u_{12} & u_{13} \\ 0 & u_{22} & u_{23} \\ 0 & 0 & u_{33} \end{bmatrix}. \]
Step 3: Set up \(A = LU\)
\[ \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 & 2 \\ 2 & 5 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 & 6 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ \ell_{21} & 1 & 0 \\ \ell_{31} & \ell_{32} & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} u_{11} & u_{12} & u_{13} \\ 0 & u_{22} & u_{23} \\ 0 & 0 & u_{33} \end{bmatrix}. \] Multiplying, \[ = \begin{bmatrix} u_{11} & u_{12} & u_{13} \\ \ell_{21}u_{11} & \ell_{21}u_{12} + u_{22} & \ell_{21}u_{13} + u_{23} \\ \ell_{31}u_{11} & \ell_{31}u_{12} + \ell_{32}u_{22} & \ell_{31}u_{13} + \ell_{32}u_{23} + u_{33} \end{bmatrix}. \]

Step 4: Compute the elements

\[ u_{11} = 4, \quad u_{12} = 2, \quad u_{13} = 2. \] \[ \ell_{21}u_{11} = 2 \Rightarrow \ell_{21} = \frac{2}{4} = \frac12, \] \[ \ell_{31}u_{11} = 2 \Rightarrow \ell_{31} = \frac{2}{4} = \frac12. \] \[ \ell_{21}u_{12} + u_{22} = 5 \Rightarrow u_{22} = 5 - \frac12(2) = 4. \] \[ \ell_{21}u_{13} + u_{23} = 1 \Rightarrow u_{23} = 1 - \frac12(2) = 0. \] \[ \ell_{31}u_{12} + \ell_{32}u_{22} = 1 \Rightarrow 1 = \frac12(2) + 4\ell_{32} \Rightarrow \ell_{32} = 0. \] \[ \ell_{31}u_{13} + \ell_{32}u_{23} + u_{33} = 6 \Rightarrow u_{33} = 6 - \frac12(2) = 5. \]

Step 5: Write the final factorization

\[ A = LU = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ \frac12 & 1 & 0 \\ \frac12 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 & 2 \\ 0 & 4 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 5 \end{bmatrix}. \]
