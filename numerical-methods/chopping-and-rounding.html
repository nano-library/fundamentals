Chopping and Rounding

Because a computer has only a finite amount of storage, most real numbers cannot be represented exactly. Instead, a real number must be replaced by a nearby number that belongs to the floating-point system. This replacement is achieved by truncating the number so that it fits within the allowed precision.

There are two standard methods for doing this: chopping and rounding.

Real Number Representation

Consider a real number of the form:

\[ x = s \cdot (.a_1 a_2 \ldots a_t a_{t+1} \ldots )_\beta \cdot \beta^e, \qquad a_1 \ne 0, \]

where the fractional expansion may contain infinitely many digits.

Let \(fl : \mathbb{R} \to F\) denote the mapping that assigns to each real number its floating-point approximation.

\[ fl(x) = \text{approximation of } x \in \mathbb{R} \text{ in the floating-point system}. \]

Chopped Machine Representation

In chopping, all digits beyond the first \(t\) fractional digits are discarded.

\[ fl(x) = s \cdot (.a_1 a_2 \ldots a_t)_\beta \cdot \beta^e. \]

Rounded Machine Representation

In rounding, the number is replaced by the nearest floating-point number. The decision depends on the digit \(a_{t+1}\).

\[ fl(x) = \begin{cases} s \cdot \big[(.a_1 a_2 \ldots a_t)_\beta + (0.00\ldots1)_\beta\big] \cdot \beta^e, & \text{if } a_{t+1} > \frac{\beta}{2}, \\[6pt] s \cdot \big[(.a_1 a_2 \ldots a_t)_\beta + (0.00\ldots1)_\beta\big] \cdot \beta^e, & \text{if } a_{t+1} = \frac{\beta}{2} \text{ and some } a_{t+s} > 0,\, s \ge 2, \\[6pt] s \cdot (.a_1 a_2 \ldots a_t)_\beta \cdot \beta^e, & \text{otherwise}. \end{cases} \]

Thus, rounding selects the closest floating-point number, whereas chopping simply removes the extra digits.

Example 1

Determine the five-digit (a) chopping and (b) rounding values of the irrational number \( \pi \).

Solution:

The number \( \pi \) has an infinite decimal expansion: \[ \pi = 3.14159265\ldots \] Written in normalized decimal form: \[ \pi = 0.314159265\ldots \times 10^1. \]

(a) Five-digit chopping

Chopping means we keep only the first five digits of the mantissa and discard the rest: \[ 0.314159265\ldots \longrightarrow 0.31415. \] Therefore, \[ fl(\pi) = 0.31415 \times 10^1 = 3.1415. \]

(b) Five-digit rounding

For rounding, we look at the digit immediately after the fifth mantissa digit. Since \[ 0.31415\underline{9}265\ldots \] has sixth digit \(9 \ge 5\), we round up. \(\left(a_6 > \frac{\beta}{2}\right)\)

When rounding to five digits, rounding up increases the fifth digit by one, which is equivalent to adding \[ 10^{-5} = 0.00001 \] to the mantissa: \[ 0.31415 + 0.00001 = 0.31416. \]

Hence the five-digit rounded floating-point value is: \[ fl(\pi) = 0.31416 \times 10^1 = 3.1416. \]
