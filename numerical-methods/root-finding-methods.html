Algebraic and Transcendental Equations

An algebraic equation is a polynomial equation where the unknown variable \(x\) is raised to a non-negative integer power and the coefficients are constants.

For example, the equation \(x^2 - x + 6 = 0\) is an algebraic equation that can be solved using various methods.

A transcendental equation involves functions such as trigonometric, logarithmic, or exponential and cannot be expressed in a specific known form. Therefore, solving transcendental equations is more challenging than solving algebraic equations.

For example, the equation \(x = tan(x)\) is a transcendental equation.

There are no general formulas to find the solution of higher degree algebraic and transcendental equations, and numerical methods are usually used to find the solution.

In many cases, these equations have no closed-form solutions, and numerical methods must be used to find approximations of the solutions.

Root-finding algorithms provide a way to find these approximations quickly and accurately.

Root Finding Process

The process of finding the roots of an equation of the form for a given function \(f\) is known as root-finding or root-solving.

A root of the equation is also called a zero of the function \(f\).

\[ f(x) = 0 \] For this process, we will encounter two types of numerical methods.
Direct methods

Methods that find the exact solution to a problem using a finite number of operations.

Iterative method

A mathematical procedure that generates a sequence of approximations to a desired solution by repeatedly applying a fixed rule. It starts with an initial approximation and then generates a new approximation in each iteration, using the result of the previous one.

The iterative method stops when a stopping criterion is met, such as reaching a certain level of accuracy or a maximum number of iterations.
