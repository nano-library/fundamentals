LU Factorization (or decomposition)

Diagonal system

The solution of a diagonal system is straightforward.

Suppose the coefficient matrix has the form

\[ \begin{bmatrix} d_1 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & d_2 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & d_3 & \ddots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & d_n \end{bmatrix} \]

Here the system \(Ax = b\) can be written

\[ d_1 x_1 = b_1 \] \[ d_2 x_2 = b_2 \] \[ \vdots \] \[ d_n x_2 = b_n \]

The solution for x i is simply \(x_i = \frac{b_i}{d_i}\).

Upper Triangular System

Let \(U\) be an upper triangular matrix. The system of linear equations \(Ux = b\) can be expressed as

\[ \begin{bmatrix} u_{11} & u_{12} & \cdots & u_{1n} & b_1 \\ & u_{22} & \cdots & u_{2n} & b_2 \\ & & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots \\ & & & u_{nn} & b_n \end{bmatrix} \]

A better idea to write \(A\) in the following form \(A = LU\), where \(L\) is lower triangular and \(U\) is upper triangular.

LU factorization

We say \(A\) has \(LU\) factorization (or decomposition) if \(A\) can be expressed as \(A = LU\), where \(L\) is a lower triangular matrix and \(U\) is an upper triangular matrix.

Note that for any invertible diagonal matrix \(D\),

\[ A = LU = L D D^{-1} U = \tilde{L}\tilde{U}. \]

Thus, LU factorisation is not unique
