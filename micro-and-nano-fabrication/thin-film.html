Thin Films

A thin film is a very thin layer of material deposited on a surface (called a substrate). Its thickness typically ranges from a fraction of a nanometer up to a few micrometers.

If we denote:

  • Film thickness as \( d_z \)
  • Lateral dimensions as \( d_x \) and \( d_y \)

For a material to be considered a thin film:

\( d_x, d_y \gg d_z \)

That means the film is extremely thin compared to its length and width. So geometrically, it is like a sheet spread over a large area.

Thin film fabrication is essentially about controlling:

  • Material flux (how many atoms arrive)
  • Energy of arriving species
  • Substrate temperature
  • Deposition rate

By tuning these parameters, we control thickness, density, microstructure, and ultimately electrical, optical, and mechanical properties.

Types of Thin Films

1. Dense Thin Films

  • Compact structure
  • Low porosity
  • High mechanical strength
  • Used in coatings, optical layers, microelectronics

2. Porous Thin Films

  • Contain voids or pores
  • Lower density
  • Very large surface area
  • Useful in sensors, catalysis, energy storage

Porous films trade mechanical density for surface area. Dense films maximize uniformity and compactness.

Thin Film Growth: Physical Methods (PVD)

Physical Vapor Deposition (PVD) involves converting a solid material into vapor and then depositing it onto a substrate as a thin film.

Common PVD Activation Methods

  • Thermal Evaporation
    Heating the source resistively so atoms evaporate.
  • Electron Beam Evaporation
    High-energy electrons heat the target locally.
  • Molecular Beam Epitaxy (MBE)
    Very controlled atomic beams deposit layer by layer.
  • Sputtering
    Argon ions bombard a target, ejecting atoms that deposit on the substrate.
  • Arc Discharge
    Plasma arc vaporizes material.
  • Laser Ablation
    A high-energy laser pulse removes atoms from the target.
