Mechanical Exfoliation/Peeling

The process of splitting a layered material into atomically thin sheets.

Mechanical exfoliation (also called the “Scotch-tape method”) means you take a layered crystal and physically peel it so that very thin sheets come off. The classic example is graphene peeled from graphite.

Mechanical exfoliation works because it exploits the big strength contrast: strong covalent bonds in-plane versus weak van der Waals bonding between planes.

Why graphite can be peeled into graphene?

  • Graphite is a stack of many graphene sheets (like a book made of ultra-thin pages).
  • Inside one sheet, carbon atoms are held by strong covalent bonds.
  • Between sheets, the attraction is weak van der Waals force, so sheets can slide and separate.

In graphite, the typical spacings shown are:

  • In-plane C-C bond length (within a sheet): \( \approx 0.14\,\text{nm} \)
  • Interlayer spacing (between sheets): \( \approx 0.34\,\text{nm} \)

Basic “tape” steps (what the image is showing)

  1. Press adhesive tape onto graphite.
  2. Peel the tape to remove thin flakes.
  3. Repeat peeling to make flakes thinner and thinner.
  4. Transfer flakes to a substrate and identify very thin regions (sometimes single-layer graphene).

What materials can use this idea?

Any material with a strong “within-layer” bonding and weak “between-layer” bonding can be exfoliated, for example graphite, MoS\(_2\), and other layered crystals.

Limitations (why this is not an industrial workhorse)

  • Large-area sheets are difficult to obtain reliably.
  • Forces are not well controlled, so thickness and quality vary (reproducibility issue).
  • Depending on the application, the purity/defects in the peeled flakes may not meet strict requirements.
