Dip Coating

Dip coating is a thin film deposition technique in which a substrate is immersed into a liquid solution and then withdrawn at a controlled speed, leaving behind a liquid layer that later dries to form a solid film.

Dip coating thickness is not controlled by a single parameter. It emerges from the balance of:

  • Viscous drag
  • Gravity
  • Surface tension
  • Evaporation rate

Control the withdrawal speed, viscosity, and evaporation, and you control the film thickness.

Basic Steps in Dip Coating

  1. Immersion - Substrate is dipped into the precursor solution.
  2. Dwell (Start-up) - Substrate remains inside to allow wetting and equilibrium.
  3. Withdrawal - Substrate is pulled upward at speed \( U_0 \).
  4. Drying (Evaporation) - Solvent evaporates, leaving a wet film.
  5. Curing (optional) - Thermal/chemical treatment to densify the film.

Film Formation During Withdrawal

As the substrate moves upward, two types of forces act on the liquid:

  • Entraining forces - Pull liquid upward with the substrate (viscous drag).
  • Draining forces - Pull liquid downward due to gravity.

The final wet film thickness is determined by the balance between these forces.

Viscous drag per unit area \( \sim \eta U_0 \)
Gravitational force per unit area \( \sim \rho g h_0 \)

Here:

  • \( \eta \) = liquid viscosity
  • \( U_0 \) = withdrawal speed
  • \( \rho \) = liquid density
  • \( g \) = acceleration due to gravity
  • \( h_0 \) = deposited film thickness

Three Film Formation Regimes

1. Drainage Regime (High Speed)

When withdrawal speed is relatively high (typically > 1 mm/s), viscous drag dominates gravity.

The film thickness is approximately:

\( h_0 = c_1 \left( \frac{\eta U_0}{\rho g} \right)^{1/2} \)

where \( c_1 \approx 0.8 \) for Newtonian liquids.

Here, solvent evaporation is slower than the motion of the drying line.

2. Landau-Levich (Surface Tension Controlled) Regime

At moderate speeds (about 1-10 mm/s), surface tension becomes important. For a Newtonian, non-evaporating liquid:

\( h_0 = 0.94 \frac{(\eta U_0)^{2/3}}{\gamma_{LV}^{1/6} (\rho g)^{1/2}} \)

where \( \gamma_{LV} \) is the liquid-vapor surface tension.

This model works well in typical dip coating conditions.

3. Capillary Regime (Very Low Speed)

At very low withdrawal speeds (< 1 mm/s), evaporation becomes faster than the movement of the drying line. Liquid rises by capillary action to replenish the film.

In this case, film thickness depends strongly on evaporation rate:

\( h_f = k_i \left( \frac{E}{L U_0} \right) \)

where:

  • \( E \) = evaporation rate
  • \( L \) = film width
  • \( U_0 \) = withdrawal speed

This regime typically applies for speeds around 0.01-0.1 mm/s.

Thickness vs Withdrawal Speed

If we combine capillary and drainage contributions, a general expression becomes:

\( h_f = k_i \left( \frac{E}{L U_0} + D U_0 \right)^{2/3} \)

This explains why:

  • At very low speeds → thickness decreases with increasing speed (capillary control).
  • At higher speeds → thickness increases with speed (viscous control).

Therefore, the thickness vs withdrawal speed curve typically shows a minimum at an intermediate speed.
