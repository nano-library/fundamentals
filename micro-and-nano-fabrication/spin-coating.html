Spin Coating

Spin coating is a simple way to make a thin, uniform film on a flat surface (like a silicon wafer). You place a few drops of liquid (solution) on the center, then spin the wafer fast. The liquid spreads outward because of the spinning, and as the solvent evaporates, a solid thin film is left behind.

Basic idea (in simple terms)

  • Spin = spread: spinning pushes the liquid outward, making it cover the surface.
  • Evaporation = solid film: solvent dries off, solute remains as a thin layer.
  • Fast spin → thinner film (usually).

Why does the liquid move outward?

During rotation, the required inward (centripetal) force is \(F = \dfrac{mv^2}{r} = m\omega^2 r\). In practice, this rotation creates strong outward flow of the liquid across the wafer, thinning the film.

Process steps

  1. Dispense: put solution drop(s) on the center of the substrate.
  2. Spin-up: accelerate to the target speed (RPM).
  3. Thinning / flow: liquid spreads outward and excess is flung off the edge.
  4. Drying: solvent evaporates and the film “sets”.
  5. (Optional) Bake: heat to remove leftover solvent and improve film quality.

What controls film thickness?

  • Rotation speed (RPM): higher speed usually gives thinner films.
  • Spin time: longer time allows more thinning + more solvent evaporation.
  • Viscosity (\(\eta\)): more viscous solutions usually give thicker films.
  • Evaporation rate (solvent): fast-evaporating solvents “lock” the film sooner.
  • Concentration (\(C_0\)): more solute generally gives thicker final film.

Thickness trend (rule-of-thumb)

A common general trend is: \[ h_f \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{\omega}} \] So if you roughly double the spin speed, thickness typically drops (not exactly by half, but it decreases).

A more specific thickness model (evaporation-limited example)

One commonly cited form (Meyerhofer-type) for final dry thickness is: \[ h_f = \left(\frac{3\eta_0 E}{2(1-C_0)\rho\,\omega^2}\right)^{1/3} \] where \( \rho \) is density, \( \eta_0 \) viscosity, \( E \) solvent evaporation rate, \( C_0 \) initial solute concentration, and \( \omega \) angular speed.

Practical tips (what usually causes failures)

  • Wetting matters: the surface should “like” the solution. If the surface is too hydrophobic for that liquid, you get beading, dewetting, and bare spots.
  • Clean substrate: dust/oil gives pinholes and thickness non-uniformity.
  • Control environment: humidity and airflow change evaporation and thickness.
  • Choose spin time by solvent: fast-evaporating solvents often need less time; slow-evaporating solvents may need longer time or a bake.

Solvent blends (why mixing helps)

  • Only low-boiling solvent: evaporates fast → easy coating, but can freeze-in a more disordered film.
  • Only high-boiling solvent: evaporates slowly → film can dewet and fling off, leaving poor coverage.
  • Mix (mostly low-boiling + some high-boiling): fast part evaporates to form the film, slow part keeps it slightly plasticized so molecules can level/organize and reduce dewetting.
