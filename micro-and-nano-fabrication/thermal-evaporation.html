Thermal Evaporation

Thermal evaporation is a Physical Vapor Deposition (PVD) method where a solid material is heated in vacuum until it evaporates, and the vapor condenses on a cooler substrate to form a thin film.

Working Principle

  • The source material is placed in a crucible inside a vacuum chamber.
  • It is heated (usually by resistive heating or electron beam heating).
  • When the material reaches sufficient vapor pressure, atoms evaporate.
  • In vacuum, these atoms travel to the substrate.
  • Atoms condense on the substrate surface → forming a thin film.

The process is simple: Heat → Evaporate → Transport → Condense

Why Vacuum is Required?

In vacuum, atoms can travel without frequent collisions. This is described using mean free path \( \lambda \).

The relation between pressure \( P \) and mean free path is:

\( \lambda = \frac{kT}{\sqrt{2}\pi\sigma^2 P} \)

  • \( k \) = Boltzmann constant
  • \( T \) = temperature
  • \( \sigma \) = molecular diameter
  • \( P \) = pressure

Important idea:

Lower pressure → larger \( \lambda \) → atoms travel in straight lines.

At high vacuum (~10-5 Torr or lower), mean free path can be meters long, larger than chamber size. So evaporation becomes highly directional (line-of-sight deposition).

Arrival Ratio

Arrival ratio = deposition rate / molecular impact rate of residual gas.

At:

  • 10-1 Torr → strong scattering (poor deposition)
  • 10-5 Torr → clean directional growth
  • 10-7 Torr and below → very high purity deposition

Thus, high vacuum improves film purity and uniformity.

Deposition Uniformity

Thermal evaporation behaves like a point source. Flux follows approximately:

\( \text{Flux} \propto \frac{1}{R^2} \)

So thickness decreases away from center. This causes:

  • Edge thinning on wafers
  • Shadowing effects
  • Difficulty in coating deep structures

Uniformity can be improved by:

  • Substrate rotation
  • Larger source-to-substrate distance
  • Using surface source instead of point source

Material Considerations

  • Best for materials with relatively high vapor pressure at moderate temperature.
  • Common metals: Au, Al, Ag.
  • Also good for many organic materials.

Example:

Aluminum boils at ~2470°C at 1 atm, but under vacuum (10-4 Torr), its evaporation temperature drops significantly (~1300°C).

Crucible Selection

  • Crucible must have higher melting/boiling point than source.
  • Must not chemically react with source material.
  • Common materials: W, Ta, alumina.

Major Limitations

  • High contamination risk from crucible or heater.
  • Not suitable for multi-component films (different vapor pressures).
  • Limited for very high melting point materials.
  • Poor step coverage due to directional nature.

Core Insight

Thermal evaporation works best when:

  • Pressure is very low (long mean free path).
  • Source has sufficient vapor pressure.
  • Line-of-sight deposition is acceptable.

It is simple, clean, and widely used — but inherently directional and not ideal for complex 3D structures.

Problem 1

Calculate the vacuum chamber pressure required to achieve a mean free path \( \lambda = 30 \text{ cm} \) for a certain vapor. Particle of diameter \(3A^\circ\). Temperature is \(300K\).

Answer:

Given relation (for the assumed conditions in the note):

\( P(\text{Torr}) \times \lambda(\text{m}) = 7.8 \times 10^{-5} \)

Convert mean free path to meters:

\( \lambda = 30 \text{ cm} = 0.30 \text{ m} \)

Now solve for pressure \(P\):

\( P = \frac{7.8 \times 10^{-5}}{\lambda} = \frac{7.8 \times 10^{-5}}{0.30} \)

\( P = 2.6 \times 10^{-4} \text{ Torr} \)

The required pressure is \( \boxed{2.6 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{Torr}} \).
